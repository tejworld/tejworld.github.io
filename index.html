About Us
Project background, model details & team
Project Introduction
HolmesLens is an intelligent crowd management system built to address the growing safety challenges at large-scale public events — concerts, sports matches, religious gatherings, and transit hubs.

Using computer vision and deep learning, HolmesLens enables security teams and operators to quantify crowd density and count individuals in real-time from live CCTV feeds, uploaded footage, or static images — without manual headcounts or expensive on-ground personnel.

The system integrates two independently-trained AI models through a lightweight Flask backend, surfaced via this desktop-optimised browser interface deployable on GitHub Pages. Alerts are generated automatically based on configurable density thresholds, giving operators actionable intelligence before situations escalate.
Model 1 — Crowd Density Estimation

What it does: Classifies crowd density from an image or video frame into four levels: Low Medium High Critical

Architecture: CNN-based regression model trained on annotated crowd images. Outputs a continuous density score (0.00–1.00) mapped to discrete alert levels via threshold rules.

Dataset: ShanghaiTech Crowd Counting Dataset (Part A & B), augmented with synthetically-generated dense and sparse scenes.

Evaluation:

MAE Mean Absolute Error on density map reconstruction
MSE Mean Squared Error per frame

Known Limitations: Performance degrades on highly occluded scenes and low-resolution inputs (<320px).

Model 2 — People Counting

What it does: Returns the exact number of people detected in a given frame using bounding-box detection.

Architecture: YOLOv8-based object detector fine-tuned on pedestrian datasets. Outputs bounding-box coordinates and a total person count per frame.

Dataset: CrowdHuman dataset combined with custom annotated frames from Indian public-gathering contexts.

Evaluation:

nAP Normalised Average Precision on pedestrian class
Recall Detection recall at IoU threshold 0.5

Known Limitations: Counts may underestimate in heavily overlapping crowds; accuracy drops in night-time or low-light scenes.

🔥 Model 3 — Fire & Smoke Detection ACTIVE

What it does: Detects presence and spread of fire and smoke in real-time from camera frames, generating immediate high-severity alerts for emergency response teams.

Architecture: YOLOv8-based object detector with two-class output (fire, smoke), fine-tuned on diverse indoor and outdoor emergency scenarios. Confidence threshold set at 0.55 for minimised false-positives.

Dataset: FIRE Dataset (Kaggle), D-Fire Dataset, and custom annotated CCTV frames from public infrastructure settings.

Evaluation:

Precision Per-class precision on fire and smoke at IoU 0.5
F1 Harmonic mean of precision and recall across detection classes

Known Limitations: May trigger false alerts on steam or strong reflections; reduced accuracy in low-light or heavily compressed video streams.

🆘 Model 4 — Person Collapse Detection ACTIVE

What it does: Identifies individuals who have collapsed or fallen and remain motionless, triggering a critical medical alert for rapid first-responder dispatch.

Architecture: Pose estimation pipeline (MediaPipe / OpenPose) combined with a temporal classifier that flags anomalous body orientation and sustained stillness over consecutive frames.

Dataset: UR Fall Detection Dataset, COCO-Pose benchmark, and custom annotated clips of fall incidents in crowd scenarios.

Evaluation:

Sensitivity True positive rate for fall / collapse events
Specificity True negative rate (low-crouch vs genuine collapse)

Known Limitations: Requires a minimum 480p feed; accuracy degrades with extreme camera angles (>70° tilt) or dense occlusion of the subject.
